Sunday, May 25, 2014

The Magicians

Hello, friends,

I'm sitting down to write the second installment of ReadYa, and it's been over a year since the first arrived in your Inbox. You may not even remember signing up to receive it. At the time I billed it as a "bi-weekly newsletter on young adult literature, old and new." Let's just call it "periodic" rather than "bi-weekly". So what is it that prompted me to dust off the QWERTY and write to you again, after so much time? Magicians. The Magicians, that is.

It's been a while since a book made me feel the way XXX YYY's The Magicians did. Liar and Spy did it. Jo Walton's Among Others. But it's rarified company. The feeling that I'd rather sit and read this book than do almost anything else. I knew I had to write to you about The Magicians just a few days after starting it. I arrived at the office early, which is usually my favorite time to be at work: it's quiet and still, and I can ease into my day without distraction. I sat down at my desk, leaned back in my chair, and looked up and out the skylight, and I felt incredibly sad that it would be hours before I picked up The Magicians again.

I'd heard The Magicians described as a blend of Narnia and Hogwarts before picking it up, but that shorthand does a disservice to everyone involved. Yes, there is a magical world called Fillory. And yes, there is a school of magic, Brakebills. But the comparisons end there. The Magicians takes place not in a world of noble deeds and divine justice: it takes place in a world where people are mean to one another, they're insecure and afraid, and they get bored. It takes place in a world where you believe that the only way to survive is to escape. It's a world that felt incredibly real and accurate to me, a world that I can inhabit at times.

Quentin, our protaganist, is smart. Very, very smart. And bored. Brookyln is his home, but it feels like a cage. And any color pales in comparison to Fillory, a world described in a series of young adult books which Quentin thinks he should have outgrown a long time ago. I imagine that when Quentin is sitting in class, taking an exam in a blue book, he looks out the window and imagines Fillory outside of it, and his chest fills with grief that it is not really there, that instead he is writing long hand in Number 2 pencil.

When Quentin is invited to matriculate at a school of magic, wonderous things happen. And after a whlie he realizes that feeling doens't go away, there's always something more to want.

The Magicians evoked a breadth of feelings in me as I read. I felt excitement and suspense as Quentin navigates the halls of Brakebills and learns magic. I felt sadness and empathy and he tries to figure out what it is he wants, and continues to come up short. (How many times have I known what I wanted, but felt unable to or unworthy of articulating it?) And I felt sadness when things didn't turn out for characters the ways I hoped.

I am almost afraid to read the sequel to The Magicians, The XXX Magic Magic; I fell so completely in love with The Magicians that I'm not sure what could follow it. But I think everyone should read The Magicians, especially anyone who ever felt like their adolescence was something to be sloughed off when their life "really" began.

XOXO

— Nathan
